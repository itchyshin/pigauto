pigauto vs TabPFN: can a tabular foundation model beat phylogeny?

Benchmark run on 2026-04-08 15:54 · 150 species · 2 reps per scenario · 25% missingness · 600 epochs

Bottom line. On simulated phylogenetic data, TabPFN does not beat the simple phylogenetic baseline, and stacking pigauto on top of TabPFN does not rescue it. The phylogenetic baseline + pigauto remains the best general-purpose approach on phylogenetic data. TabPFN is only competitive when phylogenetic signal is weak (OU with strong equilibrium pull), in which case it does win by ~13%. This suggests a hybrid worth exploring: detect the per-trait phylogenetic signal and route low-lambda traits to TabPFN.

Executive summary

  • Phylogenetic baseline is very hard to beat on phylogenetic data. Across 5 scenarios (2 reps each), TabPFN alone is worse than the baseline in 3 of 4 evaluated scenarios, often catastrophically (BM −99.8%, regime shift −122.2%).
  • pigauto (phylo + GNN) ties or marginally beats the baseline on every scenario (-0.2% to +1.9% continuous RMSE).
  • Stacking pigauto on top of TabPFN barely changes TabPFN’s predictions (<0.5 pp in every scenario). The GNN cannot recover phylogenetic signal from a baseline that has already collapsed toward the mean.
  • One scenario favours TabPFN: OU with a strong equilibrium pull, where phylogeny is a weaker predictor and cross-trait patterns matter more. TabPFN alone is +12.9% vs baseline; stacking adds another 0.5 pp.
  • Discrete traits are unchanged: pigauto matches the baseline exactly on binary and categorical accuracy (69.4% on the mixed scenario), confirming the v4 calibration fix held.

Per-scenario breakdown

Scenario: BM

Pure Brownian motion. Phylogeny is the entire story. TabPFN has no phylogenetic features so it collapses to the trait mean (RMSE ≈ 1.0 on standardised data).

Scenario: OU

Ornstein–Uhlenbeck with strong equilibrium pull. Species values are weakly dependent on phylogeny; information shared across traits dominates. This is the only scenario where TabPFN beats the phylogenetic baseline (+12.9%), and stacking adds a further 0.5 pp.

Scenario: nonlinear

Non-linear trait transform on BM. The phylogenetic baseline still extracts most of the signal; TabPFN finds no useful cross-trait pattern and is ≈38% worse.

Scenario: regime_shift

Step change at an internal node. Phylogenetic label propagation nails this because neighbours on the tree share regime. TabPFN has no tree and fails dramatically (−122%).

Scenario: mixed

Continuous + binary + categorical traits. pigauto holds the phylo baseline's continuous RMSE and fully preserves discrete accuracy (69.4%, unchanged). TabPFN errored on this scenario (a column-naming bug in the subprocess wrapper when dummy-encoded categorical columns are present).

What this means for pigauto

Two ideas worth taking seriously:

  1. pigauto is doing its job as a phylogenetic imputer, but the ceiling imposed by the phylo label-propagation baseline is high and the GNN is only finding small residual corrections (+0 to +2% RMSE). The recent calibration work means we no longer sacrifice discrete accuracy, so the method is safe to recommend as a drop-in upgrade to BACE/Rphylopars-style methods.
  2. TabPFN is the wrong default for phylogenetic data. It treats species as exchangeable rows, which destroys the tree structure that dominates these scenarios. The single exception (OU) points at a useful selector: per-trait Pagel’s λ. When λ is low, route to TabPFN; when high, use phylo+GNN. This is a much more promising architecture than blind stacking.

Bug to fix

The TabPFN subprocess wrapper errored on the mixed scenario with length of 'dimnames' [2] not equal to array extent. The cause: X_scaled has one-hot columns for the categorical trait, so ncol(mu) > length(trait_names). Fix: use colnames(data$X_scaled) rather than data$trait_names when re-attaching dim-names.
